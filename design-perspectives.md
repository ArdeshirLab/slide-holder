# Designing the Slide Holder

## Initial Thoughts/Goals
PLA Plastic Filament is a sturdy material when properly layered in the printing of 3D models. A tradeoff for this strength is potential brittleness depending on the shape of a particular model/model part, and so a balance has to be struck when making a model: the model (or slide holder, 
in this instance) should be thick enough in its geometry to avoid any premature breakage while ensuring a reasonable amount of filament is used per print. This model was printed using a Bambu A1 Mini, which estimated ~35g of filament usage per total print. 

 
## End-User Considerations
In creating the slide holder, an important aspect of design was ensuring that it was both easy to hold with any conventional laboratory gloves, and that both parts were capable of detatching with minimal effort. An initial idea for the model involved a single part, with a hole on one end to allow for entry of the slide and subsequent outlining, in the style of a rack (or a slide going into a projector). Although this would save on plastic and any struggles in part movement, a drawback would be the difficulty in making the holder both reasonably sized for the hands of different users in both inserting and removing the slide. 

## Variations in Etch-shaping 
Another area of interest in the designing of the slideholder was the shape the upper part should provide for drawing the rectangular region used by the SMI. A number of different approaches were considered, which is why there are several upper-part files. The first was a generic rectangle, scaled to compensate for the usage of a Sharpie fine-tip (not to be confused with ultra-fine point) permanent marker. A somewhat challenging aspect to designing for Sharpie use was in accounting for different drawing styles and tip sharpness: a blunt tip would mean a lower draw, and heavier reliance on the plastic tip holder. Several of the designs posted in this repository account for different approaches to outlining.
